About

From neural signal to real-world action. I work every step of that path.

My research asks how the brain commands the body to act in the real world — and how we can read that command from outside the skull. Answering it is the foundation for non-invasive brain–computer interfaces: recording neural signals with EEG, decoding intent, and closing the loop with touch and movement.

I founded and lead the University of Birmingham's markerless Movement Lab. At Meta, I helped build the hand tracking now integral to Quest. And as Co-Founder & Director of Motion Dynamics, I turn the science into hardware, haptics and XR software — then teach it to other scientists.

Meta · Reality Labs

Hand tracking.
Imperceptibly precise.

At Meta, I characterised the noise and jitter of camera-based hand tracking — then used psychophysical methods to measure exactly what the eye can detect. Those thresholds informed the filtering that holds jitter below perceivable levels, in the hand-tracking pipeline that ships on every Quest headset. The same discipline — pulling clean signal out of noise — is what makes non-invasive neural decoding possible.

Research

Four threads. One interface.

The science that makes a non-invasive brain–computer interface work.

01

Non-invasive neural recording

Reading motor plans from the scalp with EEG — how intentions are formed, predicted and corrected while people act in real-world tasks.

02

Decoding movement intent

Turning noisy cortical signals into a reliable read-out of what the body is about to do — the heart of a usable BCI.

03

Closed-loop touch

How the hand senses contact, force and texture — and how tactile feedback closes the loop and shapes the next movement.

04

Motion capture as ground truth

Markerless mocap and hand tracking measure the action the brain produced — the reference every decoder is trained against.
